Round 2 goal
- Push past the current low-frequency bias and test whether the analysis can also recognize higher-frequency fundamentals such as ~12 Hz and ~16.65 Hz.
- Avoid relying only on raw time-domain fit R^2, since weak but real lines can have tiny R^2.
- Start moving from single-frequency checks toward family-level evidence.

Known concerns entering this round
- The current score is strong on 3.35 Hz and does separate 18 Hz, but it favors strong low-frequency families.
- Higher-frequency fundamentals may be unfairly penalized because:
  - absolute time-domain amplitudes are smaller
  - local fits explain less total variance
  - broad score windows around strong low-frequency modes can dominate scans
- Cross-bond amplitude coherence and frequency stability may be more informative than absolute fit strength for weak fundamentals.

Planned tests
- Compare 3.35, 6.35, 8.96, 12.0, 16.65, and 18.0 under the same calibrated settings.
- Add metrics that do not lean too hard on raw R^2:
  - local frequency stability across regions
  - cross-bond frequency agreement within each region
  - cross-bond amplitude coherence
  - bond-shape stability across regions
  - local spectral prominence / contrast in the FFT near the target band
- Check whether 12 and 16.65 inherit a coherent “mode family” signature even if they remain weak in absolute R^2.
- Keep tracking results across other datasets to see whether these diagnostics generalize.
