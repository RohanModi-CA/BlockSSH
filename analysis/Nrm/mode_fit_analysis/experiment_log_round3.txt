Round 3 goal
- Remove the fixed-bond bias from the current analysis.
- Check whether higher-frequency fundamentals become clearer once the response bond is allowed to vary.
- Build a family-level scan across multiple datasets and components, rather than only testing preselected targets on one observable.

Hypothesis entering this round
- The current method may be unfairly weak on higher-frequency fundamentals because it always treats bond 0 as the response channel.
- For higher modes, one bond can sit near a node while another bond carries the line much more clearly.
- A better test is to scan candidate frequencies and, at each one, let the best leave-one-out response bond emerge from the data.

Planned work
- Build a bond-agnostic family scan over `IMG_0681_rot270`, `IMG_0680_rot270`, and `CDX_10IC`.
- Run that scan on `x`, `y`, and `a` components.
- For each candidate frequency:
  - fit local sinusoids by bond and region
  - compute leave-one-out scaling metrics for every possible response bond
  - keep the strongest response bond rather than forcing bond 0
  - cluster the score curve into candidate mode families
- Compare where the known reference neighborhoods land:
  - 3.35
  - 6.35
  - 8.96
  - 12.0
  - 16.65
  - 18.0

Update after implementing the bond-agnostic family scan
- Added `run_mode_family_scan.py`.
- Main change:
  - the scan no longer forces bond 0 to be the response channel
  - at each candidate frequency, every bond is tested as the response bond against a leave-one-out proxy built from the other bonds
  - the best response bond is allowed to emerge from the data
- Also fixed a compute mistake in the first version:
  - I was recomputing FFTs inside every target evaluation
  - FFTs are now cached per region and bond

What the bond-agnostic family scan changed
- On `IMG_0681_rot270 | x`, the label-free family scan now recovers:
  - `3.3 Hz`
  - `6.3 Hz`
  - `9.0 Hz`
  - `11.3 Hz`
  - and a weaker `16.4 Hz` family
- This matters because `11.3-12.0` and `16.4-16.7` were not showing up cleanly when the analysis was forced through bond 0.

Concrete results from `family_scan_output/summary.txt`
- Near `12.0 Hz`:
  - `IMG_0680_rot270 | y`: score about `1.458`, corr about `0.990`, slope about `1.125`
  - `CDX_10IC | x`: score about `1.255`, corr about `0.981`, slope about `1.158`
  - `IMG_0681_rot270 | x`: score about `1.158`, corr about `0.993`, slope about `1.082`
- So `12 Hz` now looks clearly mode-like in multiple independent views, without assuming it first.

- Near `16.65 Hz`:
  - `CDX_10IC | x`: score about `0.847`, corr about `0.935`, slope about `1.117`
  - `IMG_0681_rot270 | y`: score about `0.756`, corr about `0.853`, slope about `0.863`
  - `IMG_0680_rot270 | y`: score about `0.645`, corr about `0.883`, slope about `1.034`
- This is the first strong sign that the earlier failure around `16.65` was at least partly a response-bond / observable-selection problem, not only a physics problem.

- Near `18.0 Hz`:
  - the scores are generally lower or comparable, and when they look decent they usually come with worse frequency stability or weaker overall family support than the stronger fundamental-like families
  - for example:
    - `CDX_10IC | x` near `16.6`: score about `0.847`
    - `CDX_10IC | x` near `18.0`: score about `0.578`
  - so in at least one dataset where the higher line is clean, the bond-agnostic scan now separates `16.6` from `18`

Current interpretation
- Hidden error number one was the broadband amplitude proxy.
- Hidden error number two was forcing the test through bond 0.
- After removing both of those errors:
  - `3.35`, `6.35`, `8.96`, and `12` all look much more mode-like
  - `16.65` is still not universally strong in every view, but it is no longer failing everywhere
  - the data now suggest that `16.65` is visible only in some observables / bond patterns, which is physically plausible for a higher mode

What still seems true
- A genuinely weak but real fundamental can still have tiny absolute time-domain `R^2`.
- So the useful evidence stack is still:
  - local frequency stability
  - leave-one-out scaling
  - bond-agnostic response selection
  - local FFT prominence
  - and agreement across multiple datasets / observables
