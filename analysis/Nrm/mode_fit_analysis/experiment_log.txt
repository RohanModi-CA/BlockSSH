Goal
- Build evidence for which peaks are fundamental without assuming the answer in advance.

What has been tried so far
- Ported the notebook workflow to standalone scripts.
- Replaced FFT-bin averaging with local sinusoid fits in frequency neighborhoods.
- Compared `default` vs `comoving` bond-spacing modes.
- Calibrated the method on 3.35 Hz.
- Tested the calibrated method on 6.35, 8.96, and 18.0 Hz.

Main lessons so far
- `comoving` is not helping the scaling test. `default` is slightly better for the main 3.35 Hz mode.
- Exact fixed-frequency fitting can badly underestimate a real line when the local best frequency drifts by a few hundredths of a hertz.
- The biggest hidden error was the amplitude proxy: single-bond `mean|x|` is broadband and mixes modes.
- A leave-one-out modal proxy built from the other x bonds works much better for 3.35 Hz.
- With `default` mode, local frequency fitting, and stronger region trimming, 3.35 Hz becomes close to ideal:
  - single-bond broadband proxy: corr about 0.991, slope about 1.36
  - leave-one-out modal proxy: corr about 0.995, slope about 1.02
- There is still one weak/contaminated region that remains an outlier, but it looks like a bad segment rather than a failure of the 3.35 Hz method.

Known-mode test under calibrated settings
- 3.35 Hz: looks strongly fundamental-like.
- 6.35 Hz: inherits similar scaling/stability pattern, weaker absolute time-domain strength.
- 8.96 Hz: also inherits similar pattern, weaker/noisier than 3.35 Hz.
- 18.0 Hz: looks much less stable and much less linear under the same test.

Current limitation
- The current evidence still depends on checking chosen target frequencies.
- We need a more label-free diagnostic that scans many candidate frequencies and asks which ones behave like coherent modes.

Next experiments to run
- Dense frequency scan with a “mode-likeness” score based on:
  - frequency stability across regions
  - cross-bond coherence
  - leave-one-out scaling linearity
  - fit quality
- Check whether known fundamentals emerge from this scan without being preselected.
- See whether weak but real lines are better separated by cross-bond coherence than by raw fit R^2 alone.

Update after running more tests
- Built a dedicated 3.35 Hz calibration script.
- Best calibration settings found so far:
  - mode: `default`
  - component: `x`
  - region trims: begin about 8 s, end about 10 s
- Under those settings:
  - 3.35 Hz bond-0 amplitude vs broadband `mean|x|`: corr about 0.991, slope about 1.36
  - 3.35 Hz bond-0 amplitude vs leave-one-out modal proxy `sqrt(A1^2 + A2^2)`: corr about 0.995, slope about 1.02
- That is the clearest evidence yet that the previous analysis failure came mostly from the x-axis proxy, not from the 3.35 Hz mode itself.

Known-mode comparison under calibrated settings
- 6.35 Hz:
  - corr about 0.982
  - slope about 1.08
  - mean time-domain fit R^2 is tiny, but frequency is reasonably stable
- 8.96 Hz:
  - corr about 0.943
  - slope about 1.21
  - also weak in raw time-domain R^2, but frequency is very stable
- 18.0 Hz:
  - corr about 0.50
  - slope about 0.52
  - very poor fit quality and much larger frequency wandering
- This is consistent with 6.35 and 8.96 behaving more like fundamentals than 18.0 under the calibrated method.

Label-free scan result
- Ran a dense frequency scan from 2 to 20 Hz with a score based on:
  - leave-one-out scaling correlation
  - closeness of slope to 1
  - cross-bond coherence
  - fit quality
  - frequency stability
- The scan naturally elevates the 3.35 family, the 6.35 family, and the 8.96 family much higher than 18 Hz.
- Caveat:
  - the dominant 3.35 mode creates a broad high-scoring plateau across neighboring target frequencies, so scan outputs should be peak-picked or clustered rather than interpreted frequency-by-frequency.

Current best understanding
- The strongest hidden error was using a broadband amplitude proxy instead of a mode-aligned proxy.
- The second important issue was using `comoving`, which is not helping this problem.
- A third issue is that weak but real lines can have tiny time-domain R^2 while still being genuinely fundamental, so fit R^2 alone is not a good classifier.
- The most promising evidence stack so far is:
  - default mode
  - local frequency fitting
  - stronger trimming away from impacts
  - leave-one-out modal proxy
  - cross-bond coherence
  - frequency stability across regions

Additional tests after that
- Built a label-free scan over 2 to 20 Hz using a “mode-likeness” score combining:
  - scaling correlation against the leave-one-out proxy
  - closeness of scaling slope to 1
  - cross-bond coherence
  - fit quality
  - frequency stability
- Result:
  - the 3.35 family comes out at the top
  - the 6.35 family and 8.96 family come out much stronger than 18 Hz
  - 18 Hz scores very poorly because its fitted frequency wanders a lot and its scaling is weak
- Caveat:
  - because the 3.35 family is strong, neighboring target frequencies around it form a broad plateau in score
  - this means the next version should cluster or peak-pick the scan output, not interpret every grid point independently

Tests that were less useful
- Cross-bond amplitude shape is fairly stable even for some weak/non-fundamental lines, so shape alone is not enough.
- Relative phase between bonds is not stable enough under the current fitting procedure to be a strong discriminator.
- So far, frequency stability plus leave-one-out scaling plus cross-bond amplitude coherence look more informative than phase.

Cross-dataset check
- Tried the same coarse mode-likeness scan on other datasets:
  - `IMG_0680_rot270`
  - `CDX_10IC`
- Result:
  - the scan does not collapse when moving away from `IMG_0681_rot270`
  - it still tends to promote a few stable low-frequency families rather than just whatever is largest in raw amplitude
  - the promoted families are dataset-dependent, which is what we would expect physically
- This is useful because it suggests the score is not only memorizing the 0681 structure.
- Caveat:
  - the broad-plateau issue remains, so clustering candidate families is still the next needed improvement.
