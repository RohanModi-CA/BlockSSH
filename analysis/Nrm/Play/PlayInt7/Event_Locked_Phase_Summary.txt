# Event-Locked Phase-Only Analysis: Lessons Learned

## The Problem with Sliding-Window Bicoherence on Impacts
Earlier approaches to finding quadratic coupling (where $f_1 + f_2 \rightarrow f_3$) relied on sliding a window across the entire dataset and looking for the maximum Bicoherence (phase-locking). We used surrogate datasets (shuffling the time segments) to calculate a p-value to prove the correlation wasn't a fluke.

**The Fatal Flaw:** The data consists of highly impulsive hits. At the exact millisecond of a hit, *every* frequency mode spikes in amplitude simultaneously. 
Standard Bicoherence is an amplitude-weighted metric. When a window contains an impact, the single sample at the peak of the impact is thousands of times larger than the rest of the window. This completely dominates the vector average. 
Because all modes spiked at the exact same time, the math falsely interpreted this "amplitude co-modulation" as a guaranteed physical phase-locking. 
The surrogate test failed to catch this because shuffling the segments destroyed the aligned amplitude spikes, making the fake data easily "losable" to the real data.

## The Solution: Event-Locked Phase Locking Value (PLV)
Following expert advice, we designed a robust, event-locked method that strips away amplitude bias:

1. **Target the Post-Impact Sweet Spot:** We extract a 15-second window immediately following each known hit.
2. **Censor the Impact Core:** We calculate the broadband energy of the triad ($f_1, f_2, f_3$) and discard the top 5% of energy samples. This completely removes the single-sample amplitude spike that breaks the math.
3. **Phase-Only Metric (PLV):** We calculate the instantaneous phase residual $\Delta\phi(t) = \phi_1(t) + \phi_2(t) - \phi_3(t)$. We drop the amplitude entirely and only look at the phase vectors $e^{i\Delta\phi(t)}$. 
4. **Ensemble Pooling:** We pool these surviving phase vectors across *all* hit events to build robust statistical power. The magnitude of the average vector is the pooled PLV.
5. **Envelope-Preserving Nulls:** To calculate a true p-value, our surrogate test applies a single, random phase shift to $f_3$ for each event. This perfectly preserves the amplitude envelopes and event timing but destroys true cross-frequency phase coupling.

## Why P-Values Often Hit 0.000
A p-value of exactly `0.000` does not mean the probability is mathematically zero. It is an empirical p-value based on $N=200$ surrogate datasets. 

Even truly random phases will accidentally produce a small non-zero PLV due to the "random walk" effect over a finite number of samples (roughly $1/\sqrt{N_{samples}}$). By testing 200 phase-randomized datasets, we map out the maximum accidental PLV we expect to see by chance.

If the real PLV is higher than *all 200* fake datasets, the calculation is $0 / 200 = 0.000$. It simply means $p < 0.005$, indicating the physical phase-locking is incredibly strong and completely survives the removal of the impact core.

## The Greedy Taxonomy Algorithm
We automated this robust PLV test to crawl the spectrum and build a Family Tree:
1. Seed the largest unexplained peak as a Fundamental.
2. Mathematically calculate all Sums, Differences, and Harmonics of the known Fundamentals.
3. Check the actual spectrum for peaks near those theoretical frequencies.
4. For any match, run the Event-Locked PLV test.
5. If $p < 0.05$, the peak is physically verified as a Child mode.
6. If a large peak cannot be explained as a Child, promote it to a Fundamental and repeat.

This successfully categorized 33 distinct peaks as 1st-generation nonlinear children from just 15 fundamentals in the `CDX_10IC` dataset.
