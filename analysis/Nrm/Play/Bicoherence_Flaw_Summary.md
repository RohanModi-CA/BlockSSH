# Inquiry: Detecting Quadratic Phase-Coupling in Impulsive Mechanical Lattices

## 1. The Physical System and Data
We are analyzing the dynamics of a 1D mechanical lattice (a chain of blocks connected by elastic/magnetic hinges). The system is highly underdamped. 
We excite the chain with a localized, impulsive "hit" and record the resulting ring-down using high-speed video tracking. 
From the tracked $(x, y, \theta)$ coordinates of each block, we extract the "comoving" bond deformations (the longitudinal stretch and transverse shear between adjacent blocks in their local reference frames). The resulting dataset consists of 10-minute time series containing several discrete, high-amplitude impulsive events followed by long linear ring-downs.

## 2. The Objective
The frequency spectrum of the chain contains dozens of distinct peaks. We want to categorize the taxonomy of the spectrum:
*   **Fundamentals:** True linear resonant modes of the chain.
*   **Children:** Nonlinear modes generated by quadratic coupling (e.g., harmonics $2f_1$, sums $f_1 + f_2$, and differences $|f_1 - f_2|$).

Given the density of the spectrum, pure amplitude coincidences ($f_1 + f_2 \approx f_3$) are common. We need a mathematically rigorous way to prove that $f_1$ and $f_2$ are actively interacting to *drive* $f_3$.

## 3. The Amplitude Approach (and its limits)
Initially, we used the averaged Welch spectra across the bonds. We applied transfer-function flattening to remove the $1/f$ baseline roll-off, which stabilized the effective quadratic coupling coefficients:
$$ k_{eff} = \frac{A_3}{A_1 \times A_2} $$
While flattening made the amplitude bookkeeping much cleaner, it does not prove physical coupling. Two independent linear modes can still coincidentally sum to the frequency of a third independent mode.

## 4. The Move to Phase (Bicoherence & Sliding Windows)
To prove physical coupling, we shifted to phase-locking metrics, specifically the **Squared Bicoherence**:
$$ b^2(f_1, f_2) = \frac{|\langle X(f_1) X(f_2) X^*(f_3) \rangle|^2}{\langle |X(f_1) X(f_2)|^2 \rangle \langle |X(f_3)|^2 \rangle} $$
**The Constraint:** In our system, the nonlinear coupling is only strong when the amplitude is high (immediately following a hit). During the long tail of the ring-down, the modes decouple and ring at their independent linear frequencies, causing their phases to drift.
If we calculate the expected value $\langle \dots \rangle$ over the entire 10-minute time series, the long, free-drifting linear tails average the bicoherence to zero. 
Therefore, we must use **sliding time windows** to search for transient moments of high bicoherence localized around the hit events.

## 5. The "Simultaneous Spike" Fatal Flaw (Amplitude Co-modulation Bias)
We implemented the sliding-window bicoherence search and backed it with a surrogate dataset test (shuffling the time segments to build a null distribution). The pipeline identified several highly significant (p < 0.05) cascade relationships.

However, we recently ran strict **negative controls** that shattered this approach. We took a known, independent fundamental mode ($f_3 = 16.61$ Hz) and forced a mathematical sum with another known fundamental ($f_1 = 8.96$ Hz) and a noise band ($f_2 = 7.65$ Hz). Because $f_3$ is an independent fundamental, it should have no phase relationship with the noise band, and the bicoherence should be near zero.
Instead, the metric returned $b^2 > 0.8$ with extremely high statistical significance.

**The Mathematical Breakdown:**
Because the data is impulsive, *every* frequency mode spikes in amplitude at the exact same millisecond (the moment of the hit). 
Within a short sliding window containing a hit, the expected value integral $\langle X_1 X_2 X_3^* \rangle$ is completely dominated by the single time-sample of maximum amplitude. 
At that specific instant, the phases freeze the complex vector in one direction. Because the amplitude of that single instant is $1000\times$ larger than the rest of the window, the "drifting" of the phase over the rest of the window contributes almost nothing to the integral.
The metric is not measuring phase-locking; it is measuring **Amplitude Co-modulation**. 

**Why the Surrogate Test Failed:**
Our surrogate test shuffled the time segments. This destroyed the simultaneous amplitude alignment in the null distribution. The real data (with aligned amplitude spikes) easily beat the unaligned garbage data, generating a false-positive high-significance p-value. When we corrected the surrogate test to *only* randomize phases while keeping the amplitude envelopes perfectly aligned, the p-values of even our best candidates jumped to $p > 0.70$ (insignificant).

## 6. Failure of Parametric AR Models
We attempted to bypass the FFT windowing issues by using a time-domain **Quadratic Autoregressive (QAR)** model. We extracted the complex analytic envelopes of $f_1, f_2, f_3$ using the Hilbert transform, and tested if a quadratic interaction term ($Z_1 \times Z_2$) significantly improved the prediction of $Z_3$ over a linear AR model using an F-test.
This suffered the exact same fate. The simultaneous amplitude spike of the envelopes dominates the least-squares regression, resulting in >99.9% false-positive confidence on the negative controls.

## 7. The Core Dilemma / Request for Advice
We are trapped in a contradictory requirement:
1.  We *must* look at the transient, high-amplitude impulsive windows, because that is the only time the physical nonlinear coupling is active.
2.  Looking at impulsive windows mathematically breaks amplitude-weighted phase metrics (Bicoherence) and envelope regressions (QAR) because the simultaneous amplitude spike dominates the integration, guaranteeing a false-positive correlation for *any* $f_1 + f_2 = f_3$.
3.  If we strip amplitude weighting entirely (e.g., using pure Phase Locking Value, $PLV = |\langle e^{i(\phi_1 + \phi_2 - \phi_3)} \rangle|$), the variance of the phase noise overwhelms the signal, and nothing passes statistical significance.

**Questions for the Scholar:**
1.  Are there established techniques for calculating bispectra or nonlinear phase-coupling in strictly impulsive, highly transient regimes where simultaneous broadband amplitude spiking is unavoidable?
2.  Is there a mathematically sound way to separate true phase-entrainment from amplitude co-modulation in a short time window?
3.  Should we abandon the period *during* the hit entirely, and instead look for a "sweet spot" slightly after the hit—where the amplitude is low enough to prevent single-sample dominance, but high enough that the quadratic forcing hasn't entirely decoupled? 
4.  Are there alternative frameworks (e.g., Volterra kernels extracted via specialized system ID, Wavelet bispectra) that handle impulsive lattice dynamics more robustly than sliding-window Welch bicoherence?
